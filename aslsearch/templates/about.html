About ASL Search

An Online Dictionary for the Princeton ASL Program

The Problem:

Only 10% of ASL users are Native users, meaning they learned ASL as their first language and grew up in a Deaf community. The remaining 90% of Deaf people experience very different introductions to language - some may first learn ASL phrases through friends, online exposure, caretakes, etc. - the diversity of these experiences resulting in many different variations of signs for any given word. Furthermore, there is not a 1-1 relationship between English words and ASL signs, as the grammar and vocabulary vary significantly. Therefore, existing ASL dictionaries can be unclear, as for each English word there may be many signed videos included, without sufficient instructions for distinct usages or contexts. Without many developers dedicated to building ASL tools, existing dictionaries also lack advanced search capabilities such as suggesting terms in order of search frequency rather than alphabetically, automatic playing and looping of videos, etc. ASLsearch will remedy these difficulties as well as serve as a customizable platform for the Princeton ASL program, allowing for additional context for ASL vocabulary beyond just a collection of videos.

The Goal:

ASLsearch will be an online dictionary tool for the American Sign Language classes at Princeton. Through this platform, students may search for and learn vocabulary through a collection of signed videos and additional information posted by linguistics professors Noah Buchholz and Daniel Maier. ASL Search is meant to be used as a supplementary resource to the ASL curriculum taught in Princeton classes specifically, as exisiting online dictionaries fall short in providing enough context and advanced techonlogical features.
